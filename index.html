Does Size Impact Cost Efficiency and Financial Performance of Hospitals?

~ Examining Regional and Ownership Patterns in U.S. Hospitals ~

DS4200 Information Presentation and Visualization | Final Project | Spring 2026 | Northeastern University

Topic Introduction

Hospitals operate at the intersection of public health and economic necessity, yet they face a growing conflict between rising service demands, rising cost of labor and drugs, and intensifying financial volatility. Investigating how hospital size specifically drives cost efficiency is critical because the traditional assumption, that larger scale automatically yields better economies of scale, is frequently challenged by the realities of modern healthcare. While bigger hospitals can spread their bills across more patients, their size can also lead to messy management and higher costs that decrease their profits.

Furthermore, size is not the only factor; its impact on financial performance is deeply intertwined with confounding variables such as regional geography, ownership structures, and patient acuity. A facility’s ability to remain solvent is often dictated as much by its payer mix and local market competition as it is by its bed count. Exploring these dynamics is essential to uncover why certain hospitals thrive while others can't experience growth. Ultimately, this analysis is necessary to identify which financial outcomes are within a hospital's control and which are dictated by its structural environment.

Data Introduction

The data for this analysis comes from the Hospital Provider Cost Report dataset that is published by the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS). The data is gathered from the hospital annual cost report information maintained in the Healthcare Provider Cost Reporting Information System (HCRIS), which collects detailed financial, operational, and utilization information from all Medicare-certified hospitals in the United States. The data does not contain all measures reported in the HCRIS, but rather includes a subset of commonly used measures.

The dataset contains information from 6103 hospitals for fiscal year 2022-2023, with 117 variables covering facility characteristics, utilization metrics, financial statements, and operational data. The 2022-2023 fiscal period provides a critical look at hospital performance during the post-pandemic recovery phase, reflecting contemporary shifts in labor costs and patient volume. Key attributes include hospital identifiers (Provider CCN, name, location), facility characteristics (bed count, ownership type, urban/rural designation), utilization data (patient days, discharges, occupancy), financial metrics (revenues, expenses, assets, liabilities, net income), and staffing information (full-time equivalent employees). We cleaned the dataset by reducing the number of variables to 30 key variables as well as only focusing on the mainland United States, which resulted in excluding any territories from the dataset. The data is publicly accessible and commonly used for healthcare policy research and hospital performance analysis.

The visual analysis in the next page will lead you through our decision of key metrics, but as a preface, we will state the key financial performance and cost efficiency metrics here as well. As we will discover in the visual analysis, we will be using the number of beds as the key measure of hospital size. For financial performance, we will be using clinical operating margin and net patient revenue per bed, and for cost efficiency, we will be using operating expense ratio. For convenience, we have defined these metrics as follows:

  • Clinical Operating Margin: (Net Income / Net Patient Revenue) | This measures what percent of money was made from actually treating patients
  • Net Patient Revenue per Bed: (Net Patient Revenue / Number of Beds) | This measures income collected from patient services divided by hospital's total bed capacity
  • Operating Expense Ratio: (Operating Expenses / Net Patient Revenue) | This measures how much profit a company retains after covering its daily operational costs
