The Impact of Hospital Size on Cost Efficiency and Financial Performance: Examining Regional and Ownership Patterns

Topic Introduction

Hospitals play a central role in public health and the broader economy, yet they operate under intense financial pressure and growing demand for services. Understanding how hospital size affects cost efficiency and financial performance is crucial in a healthcare system facing rising costs, workforce shortages, and increasing demand for services. Hospital size can influence economies of scale, bargaining power, and resource allocation. Larger hospitals may spread fixed costs over more patients, potentially improving efficiency, but they may also face higher administrative complexity and operating expenses. By examining indicators such as cost to charge ratio, net patient revenue, net income, and clinical operating margin, we can better understand whether bigger hospitals are truly more financially stable and operationally efficient than smaller hospitals. Additionally, this relationship may differ by region (rural vs. urban) and hospital type (non-profit vs. for-profit) due to variations in patient mix, funding structures, and market competition. Exploring these dynamics is essential for hospital administrators to design policies and management strategies that balance both financial sustainability and high-quality healthcare. Ultimately, the results of this analysis provide a roadmap for more equitable resource allocation. By identifying the specific size thresholds or control types that struggle most with clinical margins, policymakers and administrators can better target financial subsidies and align funding with actual bed capacity. These insights help determine the most sustainable bed-to-staff ratios for different hospital sizes, ensuring that smaller facilities aren't overextended and larger systems aren't plagued by administrative bloat. This data-driven approach allows for strategic interventions that ensure hospitals of all sizes can maintain high-quality care without compromising their long-term financial viability.

Data Introduction

The data for this analysis comes from the Hospital Provider Cost Report dataset that is published by the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS). The data is gathered from the hospital annual cost report information maintained in the Healthcare Provider Cost Reporting Information System (HCRIS), which collects detailed financial, operational, and utilization information from all Medicare-certified hospitals in the United States. The data does not contain all measures reported in the HCRIS, but rather includes a subset of commonly used measures.

The dataset contains information from 6103 hospitals for fiscal year 2022-2023, with 117 variables covering facility characteristics, utilization metrics, financial statements, and operational data. The 2022-2023 fiscal period provides a critical look at hospital performance during the post-pandemic recovery phase, reflecting contemporary shifts in labor costs and patient volume. Key attributes include hospital identifiers (Provider CCN, name, location), facility characteristics (bed count, ownership type, urban/rural designation), utilization data (patient days, discharges, occupancy), financial metrics (revenues, expenses, assets, liabilities, net income), and staffing information (full-time equivalent employees). The data is publicly accessible and commonly used for healthcare policy research and hospital performance analysis.
