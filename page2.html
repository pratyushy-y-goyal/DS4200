Visual Analysis

This correlation matrix reveals the relationships between the different variables that we work with and analyze throughout this project. During our analysis, we explore both strong and weak correlations and their relationships with categorical, non-quantitative data. The strongest relationship highlighted by this visualizaiton is between Total Assets and Total Liabilities with a score of 0.91, meaning that hospitals with more assets tend to carry more liabilities, reflecting that growth is often financed through leverage. While a strong correlation, this relationship does not povide meaningful insights on the financial sustainability of hospitals within the United States, so we do not soend time analyzing this relationship later in our graphs. The second strongest correlation is between Number of Beds and Net Patient Revenue with a score of 0.84, meaning that bed count is a strong indicator for revenue for hospitals across the United States. Notably, Cost per Discharge shows almost no correlation with hospital size, challenging the assumption that larger hospitals are more efficient on a per patient basis, which is a relationship that is explored further through the cost-to-charge ratio analysis. Similarly, Operating Margin has almost no correlation with any other numerical metric. However, we will later explore the relationship between Operating Margin and different categorical variables.

Now that we’ve established variables, this visualization aims to look at the relationship between the hospital’s clinical operating margin with the type of control and the hospital size (based on number of beds). The clinical operating margin is the net income from service to patients divided by the total revenue. In other words, it represents what percent of money was made from actually treating patients. This is relevant to look at in comparison to the hospital’xs control type and size because the clinical operating margin is not only explained by the hospital size as expected by economic theories but the control type may also play a factor. The first key takeaway is that a larger hospital size does not automatically guarantee a higher clinical operating margin. While the theory of economies of scale suggests that more beds should lower costs and increase revenue per patient, the Jitter Plot reveals that this efficiency doesn't always translate into profit. Large hospitals are not always associated with a higher clinical margin because institutional factors, like the hospital's mission, can override the financial benefits of being big.The second takeaway is that proprietary hospitals, which are those typically run by corporations for profit, cluster more towards a higher clinical margin especially for those that are partnership or corporation based. Finally, for the government owned hospitals, they are clustered towards the negative clinical operation margin. Public and county hospitals face the highest financial risk. Because they often serve as "safety nets" for uninsured or underinsured patients, their clinical operating margins are volatile and frequently negative.

Now that we’ve looked at how control types contribute to hospital cost efficiency and financial performance as well as size, we can start to look at how hospitals perform regionally. This visualization aims to to look at the relationship between hospital size (via number of beds) and the cost to charge ratio among different regions in the United States. The cost to charge ratio (CCR) is the total operating costs of the hospital divided by the amount that they bill for the services. In other words, a high CCR (above 1) means that the costs for that hospital are a lot more than they are charging and a low CCR means that the hospital is charging a lot more than what the service actually costs (which is how they make a profit). The first key takeaway from this visualization is that generally as the number of beds increases, the CCR decreases, following the evidence of economies of scale. Small hospitals are financially volatile; while some are efficient, many of the dots have ratios above 0.8. The second key takeaway is the rural hospitals (blue) dominate the high CCR zone, while the urban hospitals (orange) are more concentrated in the low CCR zone. For the same hospital size, looking at the 0-200 bed range, the urban hospitals are seen mostly concentrated at the top, while rural hospitals, although seen towards the bottom as well, predominantly stay towards the top. This indicates region plays a role in the hospital cost efficiency as being a small hospital seems to be even harder in a rural area most likely because there is lower patient volume and higher difficulty recruiting staff.
