Figure 1: Heatmap of Staffing Density Across Hospital

Interaction: Hover over the heatmap to see the density of hospitals with specific combinations of bed count and FTE employees.

Takeaway:

This visualization explores the relationship between the number of beds in a certain hospital representing its size and FTE (full time employees) on payroll at the hospital. Concentrated in the lower-left, the data shows that most U.S. hospitals are small facilities (< 250 beds) with lean staffing. The zoomed-in graph more specifically shows this with most hospitals in the U.S. having a range of 20-30 beds and 100-200 employees. While FTEs generally scale with bed count, the distribution fans out significantly between 200–500 beds, suggesting that size alone does not dictate staffing structures. Upper-left outliers likely represent academic medical centers, where research and specialized care require staffing levels that exceed standard patient volumes. Overall, the visualization demonstrates that generally the number of beds and FTE are correlated with one another; thus, when choosing the variable to represent hospital size, we will be using the number of beds as it is more commonly used in healthcare research

Figure 2: Heatmap Feature Correlation Matrix

Interaction: Hover over the heatmap to see the correlation values between different features.

Takeaway:

This correlation matrix is used to determine which variables will be used for the subsequent analysis, depending on their relationship to the number of beds. Number of beds shows strong positive correlations with FTE Employees on Payroll (0.84), and Net Patient Revenue (0.84). Conversely, certain variables show nearly no correlation with bed count; specifically, the operating expense ratio (-0.02) and clinical operating margin (0.02) remain stagnant regardless of size, meaning that scaling up a facility does not automatically translate into better profit margins. This challenges the common assumption that bigger hospitals benefit from economies of scale in ways that reduce per-patient costs or boost margins. Instead, these metrics appear to be driven by factors that bed count alone cannot capture. This requires a deeper investigation into what metrics provide a better explanation to the variation in profitability and cost efficiency. Based on these results, we will explore the relationship between bed count and clinical operating margin, net patient revenue per bed, and operating expense ratio in the subsequent visualizations.

Figure 3: Boxplot of Clinical Operating Margin by Type of Control and Size Category

Interaction: (1) Hover over any boxplot to view specific quartile and median data. (2) Use the radio buttons to isolate a specific hospital size for a direct ownership-type comparison.

Takeaway:

Our initial heatmap analysis revealed that the number of beds and clinical operating margin were largely uncorrelated; this visualization supports those initial results. The visualization suggests that the ownership type (i.e. the hospital’s mission) is a better predictor of hospital financial performance than the number of beds. There is subtle evidence of economies of scale, which is when the cost per patient decreases as the hospital gets larger, demonstrated where larger facilities cluster slightly further to the right than smaller ones in the proprietary rows. However, the size trend is secondary to the overall ownership pattern. The visualization has proprietary hospitals showing a dense concentration in the positive margin (generating profit from patients) regardless of bed count, whereas government-owned hospitals are consistently clustered in the negative zone, suggesting that because public hospitals must treat everyone regardless of cost, increasing the number of beds will not mitigate that cost.

Figure 4: Scatterplot showing the relationship between Operating Expense Ratio and Number of Beds, colored by Region

Takeaway:

Another variable that we saw was uncorrelated with the number of beds was operating expense ratio. While ownership has been established as a primary driver of financial performance, this suggests that variables beyond hospital size may also exert significant influence on cost efficiency. One of the variables that we are curious to look at is regional differences, specifically how operating expense ratio differs between rural and urban hospitals. The data reveals that operating expense ratios remain largely stagnant regardless of hospital size, reinforcing our initial findings. However, the geographic variance in the plot suggests that a hospital's region may play a bigger role on hospital efficiency than size alone. This is most visible in the 0–200 bed range, where rural hospitals (blue) predominantly occupy the "high-expense" zone above the 1.0 break-even line, while urban hospitals (orange) cluster below the 1.0 line, representing greater hospital efficiency. Thus, hospital size alone does not guarantee efficiency; rather, urban location provides a volume-driven density advantage that rural facilities cannot replicate simply by adding beds.

Figure 5: Choropleth Map of Average Net Patient Revenue per Bed by State

Interaction: (1) Hover over each state to see the average net patient revenue/bed for that state. (2) Click on the state see the revenue distribution of all hospitals within that state

Takeaway:

While the rural-urban split provides a broad baseline, we can obtain a more refined understanding of these geographic pressures by examining performance at the state level. Building on the finding that region drives cost efficiency, we want to see if geography exerts a similar influence on net patient revenue; even though revenue is tied to size, we want to know if location significantly dictates financial performance. As such, the map suggests each state has a revenue ceiling, which is the maximum amount of revenue a business can generate given its current capacity. We can see a massive disparity between the high-revenue Pacific Northwest and Northeast and the low-revenue areas of the South, like Louisiana and Mississippi. While ownership defines a hospital’s goals and size provides the scale, the regional environment ultimately defines the opportunity for survival. If a hospital is in a low-revenue state, it doesn't matter how large it grows or how much its owners want to make a profit, the money simply isn't there to be collected.

Figure 6: Sankey Diagram of Hospital Size, Financial Health, and Revenue Performance

Interaction: (1) Hover over each line to see percentage of hospitals within that flow. (2) Reposition the vertical bars to visually compare flow widths

Takeaway:

This final Sankey diagram connects hospital size with key financial performance metrics, including operating expense ratio, clinical operating margin, and net patient revenue. The visualization shows that hospital size is not the primary factor driving cost efficiency or financial performance. If size were the main driver, we would expect a clear pattern where small hospitals consistently experience low margins and low revenue, and large hospitals consistently achieve high margins and high revenue. Instead, the data reveals a more complex relationship. Hospitals of all sizes appear across both strong and weak financial outcomes, indicating that size alone does not determine performance. While larger hospitals show a somewhat stronger tendency toward better outcomes, there are still many that experience low margins and revenue. This suggests that financial efficiency, particularly operating expense ratio, plays a more important role than size, along with other factors that influence hospital performance.

Conclusions

The data consistently proves that just because hospitals are large does not necessarily mean they are financially successful. In other words, simply adding more beds does not solve the underlying economic math that governs a hospital’s survival. Instead, we must address the regional disparities in net patient revenue and the ownership controls that force public and rural facilities into high-risk financial volatility. While a proprietary urban hospital might find success through market density, a rural government-owned facility is often trapped by a state-level revenue ceiling that no amount of expansion can break. To preserve essential healthcare infrastructure, the focus must shift from physical expansion to state-level policy changes, such as Medicaid reform and targeted rural subsidies, that address the geographic and mission-driven hurdles these institutions face. In other words, a hospital’s survival is determined less by the number of beds and more by the economic and political boundaries that surround them.

Future Work

While this analysis provides a critical baseline, the scope of the project was necessarily limited by the available variables and the cross-sectional nature of the dataset. Future research should integrate a longitudinal perspective, comparing hospital performance across multiple years to assess how shifting state-level policies, such as Medicaid expansion or changes in rural subsidy structures, impact financial trajectories over time. Additionally, it is essential to explore other financial metrics to determine if hospital size exerts an influence that wasn't apparent in this initial study. Beyond size, integrating simpler operational factors like facility age, payer mix, and the number of specialized departments would provide a more nuanced understanding of the true drivers of cost efficiency/financial performance. Ultimately, this would help us see whether a hospital's financial struggle is a permanent fact of its location or a problem that can be fixed with the right policy changes.
