Visual Analysis

Figure 1: Staffing Density Across Hospital Sizes

This visualization explores the relationship between the number of beds in a certain hospital representing its size and FTE (full time employees) on payroll at the hospital. The density of hospitals is heavily concentrated in the lower-left corner of the heatmap, reflecting that the majority of U.S. hospitals are small community facilities with less than 250 beds and correspondingly lean staffing levels. As bed count increases, FTE rises alongside it, but the distribution begins to fan out noticeably around the 200-500 bed range, where variability in staffing decisions becomes much greater, suggesting that hospitals of similar size can operate with vastly different workforce structures, and that size alone does not determine staffing efficiency. Outliers in the upper-left region, with high FTE relative to bed count, likely represent academic medical centers where staffing demands extend well beyond typical patient volume into research and specialized care. More importantly, this wide spread in the data reveals that there is no universal "right" ratio of beds to staff, and that rural and smaller hospitals are often required to maintain minimum staffing thresholds regardless of patient volume, creating fixed labor costs that cannot be offset through expansion. This reinforces a key finding across all visualizations that scaling up physical capacity does not translate into financial stability, and the staffing burdens facing smaller, more rural facilities are structural in nature, demanding policy solutions rather than physical ones. To understand the financial consequences of these structural pressures, the following visualizations examine how different metrics, both numerical and categorical, reveal the deeper economic fault lines that staffing patterns alone cannot capture.

Figure 2: Heatmap Correlation Matrix

This correlation matrix reveals the relationships between the different variables that we work with and analyze throughout this project. During our analysis, we explore both strong and weak correlations and their relationships with categorical, non-quantitative data. The strongest meaningful relationship highlighted by this visualization is between Total Costs and Net Patient revenue with a score of 0.97, indicating that revenue and costs scale almost perfectly together across hospitals. Number of Beds is also strongly correlated with both Total Costs and Net Patient Revenue with respective scores of 0.86 and 0.84, meaning that bed count is a strong indicator of both the scale of operations and revenue generation. Notably, Cost per Discharge shows almost no correlation with hospital size, challenging the assumption that larger hospitals are more efficient on a per-patient basis, which is a relationship we explore further through the cost-to-charge ratio analysis. Clinical Operating Margin and Operating Expense Ratio show a perfect inverse correlation of -1.00, as they are derived from the same underling values, so we exclude this relationship from further analysis. On the other hand, both margin metrics have almost no correlation with any other numerical variable. However, we will later explore the relationship between Clinical Operating Margin and different categorical variables.

Figure 3: Jitter Plot of Clinical Operating Margin by Type of Control and Size Category

This jitter plot investigates the Clinical Operating Margin, which is the percentage of profit derived specifically from patient care, to uncover the drivers of financial health that hospital size failed to explain. Our initial heatmap analysis revealed that Bed Count and Operating Margin were largely uncorrelated; this visualization identifies Ownership Type as the primary factor filling that gap. The data confirms that an hospital's mission is a far more potent predictor of margin than its size: while there is subtle evidence of economies of scale within specific categories, as demonstrated where larger facilities (blue circles) cluster slightly further to the right than smaller ones (dark orange circles) in the proprietary rows. However, the size trend is secondary to the overall ownership patterns. Proprietary hospitals show a dense concentration in the positive margin range regardless of bed count, whereas government-owned hospitals are consistently clustered in the negative zone, suggesting that because public hospitals must treat everyone regardless of cost, they face financial limits that even large-scale operations cannot overcome. Ultimately, the plot demonstrates that because clinical operating margin does not respond significantly to increases in size, ownership type could be an explanatory factor for a hospital's financial trajectory

Figure 4: Scatterplot showing the relationship between Operating Expense Ratio and Number of Beds, colored by Region

Now that we’ve established that ownership is a factor that determines financial performance, we also want to explore how the region affects the day-to-day cost pressures hospitals face. This scatterplot examines the relationship between hospital size and the Operating Expense Ratio (OER), encoding each point based on region. The OER is a metric to determine how much profit a company retains after covering its daily operational costs. We chose to analyze OER because, like our earlier clincial operating margin analysis, it showed no strong correlation with size, which suggests other factors might be involved. Even within the same ownership category, we see a wide spread of performance, suggesting that while ownership defines the strategy of the hospital, the region defines the opportunity. A "for-profit hospital" in a struggling rural area faces fundamentally different hurdles, such as low patient volume and high recruitment costs, than one in a thriving urban center. This is most visible in the 0–200 bed range, where rural hospitals (blue) predominantly occupy the "high-expense" zone above the 1.0 break-even line, while urban hospitals (orange) cluster toward the more efficient bottom. The big takeaway here is that being "big" does not make a hospital efficient; rather, being "urban" provides a volume-driven density advantage that rural hospitals simply cannot access, regardless of how many beds they add.

Now we are going to look at a more refined view of the regional trend by looking at the average Net Patient Revenue per Bed by state. Because our initial research showed that total revenue was highly correlated with the number of beds, we normalized the data to see the true value of a single bed in different parts of the country without the bias of hospital size. This map completes the puzzle by showing that each state has a revenue ceiling, which is the maximum amount of revenue a business can generate given its current capacity. We can see a massive disparity between the high-revenue Pacific Northwest and Northeast and the deep blue areas of the South, like Louisiana and Mississippi. These low-revenue states often have higher concentrations of rural hospitals that are already struggling with high costs and a large number of uninsured patients. The big takeaway here is that geography creates a hard revenue ceiling that overrides everything else; while ownership defines a hospital’s goals and size provides the scale, the regional environment ultimately defines the opportunity for survival. If a hospital is in a low-revenue state, it doesn't matter how large it grows or how much its owners want to make a profit, the money simply isn't there to be collected.

Conclusions

The data consistently proves that we cannot expect hospitals to "size" their way out of a financial crisis. Because profitability is almost entirely decoupled from scale, simply adding more beds does not solve the underlying economic math that governs a hospital’s survival. Instead, we must address the Regional Disparities in revenue and the Ownership Mandates that force public and rural facilities into high-risk financial volatility. While a proprietary urban hospital might find success through market density, a rural government-owned facility is often trapped by a state-level revenue ceiling that no amount of expansion can break. To save the healthcare system, the focus must shift from physical expansion to state-level policy changes, such as Medicaid reform and targeted rural subsidies, that address the geographic and mission-driven hurdles these institutions face. Ultimately, if the goal is a stable healthcare system, we must stop building bigger hospitals and start building better policies that protect the facilities we already have.

Sankey Diagram

This graph shows how hospital size connects to financial health and then to revenue performance. Small hospitals are more likely to move toward lower margin and lower revenue groups. Medium hospitals are more mixed, which means their results are not all the same. Large hospitals are more likely to move toward higher margin and higher revenue groups. This suggests that hospital size has a strong link with financial results. Bigger hospitals may have more resources, more patients, and more ways to spread costs, which can help them perform better. Smaller hospitals may face more pressure because they have fewer beds and fewer resources to handle costs. The graph also helps us see that financial health sits in the middle of this pattern. In other words, size affects margin, and margin is connected to revenue performance. This makes the chart useful because it does not only show one simple relationship. It shows a path from size to money outcomes. The main takeaway is that hospital size matters, and larger hospitals tend to be in a stronger financial position than smaller hospitals.
